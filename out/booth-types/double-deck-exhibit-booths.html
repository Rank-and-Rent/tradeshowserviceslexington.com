Double-Deck Exhibit Booths

Double-deck exhibit booths in Lexington carry a second-floor meeting or hospitality area, which means structural engineering, fire marshal review, longer install windows, and a different conversation with the venue's exhibitor services desk.

Double-decks are the most operationally demanding booth type. The second floor is real construction: stamped engineering, code review, weight loading, and exit path planning all become part of the project.

Lexington double-decks work at Central Bank Center where ceiling height and structural rules support the build. Hotel ballrooms generally don't support a second floor, which constrains where the booth can land.

Operationally, the install window is longer (3 to 5 days versus 1 to 2 for a comparable single-story island), the labor calls are bigger, and the rigging plan is more complex.

These pages cover what a double-deck scope includes, how the engineering and code review fits the timeline, and where the second-floor hospitality investment actually pays back.

Double-Deck Exhibit Booths priorities

  • Keep Double-Deck Exhibit Booths tied to the actual show outcome, not a vague category label.
  • Treat the venue manual, freight timing, and access plan as the baseline for the build.
  • Align graphics, AV, and utility requests with the same calendar as the booth work.
  • Leave room for storage, reuse, and return freight so the next cycle starts cleanly.
  • Give the floor one clear lead so live decisions stay controlled during install and strike.

Lexington trade show market

The Lexington market for double-deck exhibit booths runs on a small set of venues and a clear regional drive radius. Central Bank Center anchors downtown; the host-hotel ring carries the breakouts; the Bluegrass corridor extends the audience.

The city's identity (university, healthcare, equine, bourbon, manufacturing) gives programs more than one buyer story to work with, which is why Lexington pulls real recurring demand instead of one-off spillover.

For double-deck exhibit booths, the practical question is which venue and which week. Peak weeks compress dock windows and labor capacity; off-peak weeks open up the calendar.

The strongest programs match the venue to the audience. A sponsor expo at Central Bank Center reads differently than a board offsite at Griffin Gate or a brand dinner at The Campbell House.

Where Lexington shows actually happen

The bulk of Lexington exhibit activity lives at Central Bank Center, with the city's largest floor and the connected ballroom and arena.

The downtown host-hotel ring around the convention campus handles breakouts, sponsor inventory, and the overflow that the convention floor pushes off-floor.

Outside downtown, Griffin Gate covers north Lexington's resort programming, while The Campbell House and Embassy Suites Lexington Green run the south-side suburban meeting belt.

Who handles what during the Lexington install window

Inside the install window for double-deck exhibit booths, the named on-site lead holds the drawing, the punch list, and the labor calls. That role doesn't rotate; it stays with one person from crate-open through final strike.

The venue's exhibitor services desk handles utility orders, rigging approvals, and the published service paperwork. The show GC (where there is one) handles drayage and the master schedule.

The labor crew runs against the venue's labor model. At Central Bank Center the crew works the building's published rules; at downtown hotels the crew works through the property's events team.

Communication runs on a daily report during install, real-time updates during show hours, and a written debrief delivered within a day of strike.

Where Lexington shows cluster

Most Lexington shows cluster downtown around Central Bank Center, the host-hotel ring along Vine, High, and Main, and the walkable restaurant and hospitality blocks around Rupp Arena.

For double-deck exhibit booths, the secondary clusters are the Newtown Pike resort corridor anchored on Griffin Gate, the south Lexington belt around The Campbell House and Embassy Suites Lexington Green, and the regional Bluegrass corridor (Versailles, Georgetown, Richmond, Frankfort, Nicholasville, Winchester) that extends the drive market.

What's included in the project scope

Scope for double-deck exhibit booths starts from the program's specific inputs: show, venue, audience, calendar. Each one shifts what the engagement covers.

Double-deck scope covers structural engineering and stamping, code and fire-marshal review, fabrication, second-floor finishes, AV and lighting integration, longer install window with rigging approvals, supervised show-hours coverage, and strike against the venue's posted dismantle plan.

Boundary lines on the scope: organizer fees, client travel, internal client software, and any preexisting vendor contracts stay outside the engagement.

Multi-show double-deck exhibit booths programs add a maintenance layer: asset storage, scheduled refurb, graphic swaps, and the planned freight loop.

Scope discipline upstream beats heroics on install day. Most install-day fires are unaddressed scope items, not crew failures.

From first call to strike

Step one on double-deck exhibit booths: the brief. Show, venue, dates, outcome all confirm before any other phase opens.

Strategy phase produces the brief; design phase produces the drawing set and the budget frame.

Phase three is venue paperwork: utilities, rigging, labor, EAC packets all file before the discount cutoffs close.

Production phase covers fabrication, graphics, AV, rentals, and the floor playbook. Dry-fit happens on the shop floor before crating.

Install honors the venue's published schedule. One named lead carries the install through show hours without rotation.

Closeout: strike, freight, debrief. The written debrief lands within a day and shapes the next show.

Budget and timeline

Budget for double-deck exhibit booths scales with how the booth is built, how big it is, what the venue charges for its services, what the graphics look like, how far the crates travel, and how many supervision days the program runs.

The line items on a typical double-deck exhibit booths budget: structure, graphics, AV, show services, labor, freight, supervision. Round numbers without that breakdown tend to drift during execution.

Timeline: Custom: 16 to 20 weeks. Rental: 8 to 12. Under 4 weeks is possible but narrows choices and raises cost.

What a successful project looks like

Successful double-deck exhibit booths executes against five tests: on-time install, first-walk inspection, quiet show hours, clean strike, documented closeout.

Real outcomes on double-deck exhibit booths sit in lead capture and pipeline data. The build and install protect those numbers; sloppy execution erodes them.

Within a week post-show, the booth's actual performance reads more clearly than any pre-show forecast. The debrief captures both technical and behavioral observations.

The advantage of a local Lexington team

For double-deck exhibit booths, working with a Lexington-based shop matters most when something goes wrong on install day. A local team with an in-house shop solves most install-day problems in the time a national vendor needs to dispatch a flight.

Local presence also means the venue relationships are real. The same person calling the exhibitor services desk at Central Bank Center has been in the building enough times to know which approvals come back fast.

Across a multi-show calendar, the compounding effect of one team running double-deck exhibit booths is fewer surprises and shorter install times. The fourth show runs on memory; the first one runs on first-principles planning.

Double-Deck Exhibit Booths planning questions

Do you work at Hyatt Regency Lexington?

Yes. the downtown Hyatt is part of the convention-hotel ring tied directly to the Central Bank Center campus. Projects at that building are planned against its specific floor rules.

How does labor work in Lexington?

It runs venue by venue. Hilton Lexington/Downtown: the Hilton downtown section makes clear that the hotel supports group travel and events across its downtown location steps from Rupp Arena and Lexington Center. Other Lexington venues have their own published rules, and the plan matches the specific building.

How much lead time is ideal?

Custom builds run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rentals take 8 to 12 weeks. Work inside 4 weeks is possible but raises costs and narrows the options.

What information kicks off a project?

The show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and a target budget range. From those, an initial scope and a realistic cost come back within a few days.

Who runs the booth on-site?

One named show-site lead runs the install, the show hours, and the strike. That person carries the floor plan and the punch list from crates to doors and reports up once a day.

Can the booth travel to the next city on our calendar?

Yes — builds are designed for modular reuse unless single-use scenic is specifically required. Same structure, swappable graphics, standard crates. Lexington programs that roll to another city on the same calendar get handled end to end.

What happens if something breaks during show hours?

The on-site lead handles it. Minor repairs happen in place; anything larger gets sourced from the local shop or rerouted through the venue's service desk.
