Scope on a event furnishing rentals project

Project scope usually spans design, fabrication, graphics, AV, labor coordination, freight, install, show-hours support, and strike.

What shows up in most budgets: structure, graphics, AV, show-service orders through the venue, labor, freight, supervision days, and closeout. Individual projects layer or remove items based on scope.

The handoffs on an event furnishing rentals project matter almost as much as the line items. Design has to hand a build-ready drawing to fabrication. Fabrication has to hand a pre-built, dry-fitted booth to crating. The crater has to hand a labeled, manifested shipment to the carrier. Every handoff is a place quality can drop.

Multi-show programs add asset management: storage, refurbishment, graphic swaps, and scheduled re-kitting so the booth rolls cleanly from Lexington to the next city on the calendar.

Scope boundary is as important as scope inclusion. Show organizer fees, client-side staff costs, and any tools the client already owns are handled outside the engagement. Defining that boundary up front prevents billing surprises later.

From first call to strike

Every project starts with a scope conversation: the show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and the budget frame. Ten minutes of specifics produces a better plan than a week of generalities.

After the brief, the work turns to the building itself. Dock windows, utility orders, rigging approvals, and labor calls are all confirmed in writing before anything goes to fabrication.

With venue details locked, production runs. Build, print, AV integration, rentals, and the show-floor coordinator's playbook all come together in this phase.

One named show-site lead runs the install. Not a rotating crew and not a remote account manager — one person holds the floor plan, the punch list, and the labor calls from crates to doors.

Throughout show hours, the show-floor coordinator stays present. Tech breaks, sponsor changes, venue requests — all handled without pulling the client team off the floor.

After the show, strike runs against the venue's posted window, and a same-day debrief captures what held and what needs to change before the next date.

Cost and lead time

Every project has the same cost drivers. The number depends on scope, and scope depends on a handful of specific variables.

The levers that matter: build path (custom, rental, or hybrid), footprint, venue service fees, graphics, freight, and supervision days. A 10x10 turnkey rental and a 30x50 custom island with a double deck are entirely different projects.

On timing: custom builds for Lexington shows run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rental programs run on 8 to 12. Anything inside 4 weeks is a rush, which means fewer options and a premium on materials.

What a successful project looks like

The measure of a good engagement is simple: on-time install, no venue callouts, clean show hours, and a closeout that makes the next event easier to book.

Exhibitors who measure programs seriously track leads, floor-booked meetings, and post-show pipeline. The build and the install are upstream inputs to those KPIs.

The post-show debrief is where the numbers get connected back to the physical booth. Which demo station drew traffic, which meeting area closed more conversations, which graphic zone attracted dwell time — that feedback shapes the next build rather than getting lost.

Event Furnishing Rentals planning questions

Do you work at Hilton Lexington/Downtown?

Yes. Hilton Lexington/Downtown sits steps from Rupp Arena and Lexington Center in the downtown core. Projects at that building are planned against its specific floor rules.

How does labor work in Lexington?

It runs venue by venue. Central Bank Center: The venue's public rules and regulations materials show that planners should work from the current event documents and contracts, including service ordering and venue coordination, rather than assuming a generic convention-center process. Other Lexington venues have their own published rules, and the plan matches the specific building.

How much lead time is ideal?

Custom builds run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rentals take 8 to 12 weeks. Work inside 4 weeks is possible but raises costs and narrows the options.

What information kicks off a project?

The show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and a target budget range. From those, an initial scope and a realistic cost come back within a few days.

Who runs the booth on-site?

One named show-site lead runs the install, the show hours, and the strike. That person carries the floor plan and the punch list from crates to doors and reports up once a day.

Can the booth travel to the next city on our calendar?

Yes — builds are designed for modular reuse unless single-use scenic is specifically required. Same structure, swappable graphics, standard crates. Lexington programs that roll to another city on the same calendar get handled end to end.

What happens if something breaks during show hours?

The show-floor coordinator handles it. Minor repairs happen in place; anything larger gets sourced from the production source or rerouted through the venue's service desk.
