Show-Site Supervision

Show-site supervision in Lexington is the named lead on the floor: one person holds the drawing, the punch list, the labor calls, and the venue conversations from crate-open through final strike.

Supervision is what turns a stack of crates into a working booth. The lead carries the drawing set, the labor schedule, the AV cue sheet, and the venue's exhibitor manual all in the same head.

On Lexington shows the supervisor talks to the venue's exhibitor services desk, the show GC, and the labor crew leads on the same floor. One person with one set of decisions.

Show hours are part of the scope, not a separate phase. The supervisor stays present for tech breaks, sponsor changes, and venue requests so the client team can keep working booth visitors.

These pages cover what supervision delivers, how the role runs across the install, show-hours, and strike, and what the post-show debrief looks like.

Show-Site Supervision priorities

  • Keep Show-Site Supervision tied to the actual show outcome, not a vague category label.
  • Treat the venue manual, freight timing, and access plan as the baseline for the build.
  • Align graphics, AV, and utility requests with the same calendar as the booth work.
  • Leave room for storage, reuse, and return freight so the next cycle starts cleanly.
  • Give the floor one clear lead so live decisions stay controlled during install and strike.

Lexington trade show market

The Lexington market for show-site supervision runs on a small set of venues and a clear regional drive radius. Central Bank Center anchors downtown; the host-hotel ring carries the breakouts; the Bluegrass corridor extends the audience.

The city's identity (university, healthcare, equine, bourbon, manufacturing) gives programs more than one buyer story to work with, which is why Lexington pulls real recurring demand instead of one-off spillover.

For show-site supervision, the practical question is which venue and which week. Peak weeks compress dock windows and labor capacity; off-peak weeks open up the calendar.

The strongest programs match the venue to the audience. A sponsor expo at Central Bank Center reads differently than a board offsite at Griffin Gate or a brand dinner at The Campbell House.

Lexington convention venues and meeting properties

Central Bank Center at 430 W Vine carries the city's largest exhibit floor and runs on its own published rules and dimensions document.

The downtown host-hotel ring (Hyatt Regency Lexington at 401 W High, Hilton Lexington/Downtown at 369 W Vine, Lexington Marriott City Center at 121 W Vine) supports breakouts, sponsor rooms, and overflow.

Griffin Gate at 1800 Newtown Pike runs as Marriott's resort with meeting venues, a golf course, and a spa, which fits retreats and hospitality-heavy programs.

South Lexington adds The Campbell House at 1375 S Broadway with more than 25,000 square feet of meeting space and Embassy Suites Lexington Green at 245 Lexington Green Cir as the suite-hotel option.

For show-site supervision, picking the right property is the upstream decision. The venue's published rules drive everything that follows: utility orders, rigging, labor, freight.

Lexington labor and venue services

Labor in Lexington follows the venue, not the city. Each property publishes its own rules, and a {label} engagement has to read them per show.

Show-site supervision labor planning is per-venue. The split between house crew and outside labor is whatever the venue publishes, not a citywide standard.

Show-services paperwork files through the venue's own portal or form, with a discount cutoff sitting 30 to 45 days before show open.

The discipline that prevents most issues: current manual in hand, orders inside the cutoff window, EAC packet filed early.

Downtown Lexington carries the convention core for show-site supervision: Central Bank Center, the surrounding host hotels, and the walkable hospitality pattern along Vine, High, and Main.

Outside downtown, show-site supervision work also runs the Newtown Pike resort corridor at Griffin Gate, the south Lexington belt around The Campbell House and Embassy Suites Lexington Green, and the regional Bluegrass towns of Versailles, Georgetown, Richmond, Frankfort, Nicholasville, and Winchester.

Scope on a show-site supervision project

A supervision scope for show-site supervision puts one named lead on the floor from crate-open through final strike.

The scope covers install lead, daily reports during build, show-hours coverage, on-floor problem solving, strike management, manifest signoff, and a written post-show debrief delivered within a day.

The supervisor doesn't replace the GC's exhibitor services desk; that desk handles venue order changes while the supervisor handles the booth's own running list.

Show hours are part of the scope, not an extra. The supervisor stays present for tech breaks, sponsor changes, and venue requests so the client team keeps working visitors.

Supervisors arrive 24 to 48 hours before doors and stay through final crate handoff to the freight company.

The process from start to finish

Phase one on show-site supervision is the brief. The show, the venue, the audience, the calendar, and the budget all go on the table first.

Brief and drawings come out of strategy and design. Budget gets framed against the venue's published rates.

Venue paperwork moves on coordination cutoffs. Utility, rigging, labor calls, EAC if applicable all file in writing.

Production runs the build through dry-fit. Catching problems on the shop floor saves labor calls at the venue.

Move-in, show hours, and move-out all run with one named lead, all against the venue's published windows.

The post-show writeup lands inside a day and feeds directly into the next planning cycle.

Budget and timeline

Budget for show-site supervision scales with how the booth is built, how big it is, what the venue charges for its services, what the graphics look like, how far the crates travel, and how many supervision days the program runs.

The line items on a typical show-site supervision budget: structure, graphics, AV, show services, labor, freight, supervision. Round numbers without that breakdown tend to drift during execution.

Timeline: Supervisors come in for the full install-through-strike window, usually arriving 24 to 48 hours before doors and staying through final crate handoff to the freight company.

Outcomes we optimize for

What a good show-site supervision engagement delivers: on-time floor-ready, no venue inspection issues, undisturbed show hours, closeout report inside a day.

Programs that take show-site supervision seriously measure leads, meetings, and pipeline. Everything the team does on build and install is upstream of those three.

Post-show review connects technical data to physical layout. Demo station performance, meeting area effectiveness, graphic dwell time, all inform the next iteration.

The advantage of a local Lexington team

For show-site supervision, working with a Lexington-based shop matters most when something goes wrong on install day. A local team with an in-house shop solves most install-day problems in the time a national vendor needs to dispatch a flight.

Local presence also means the venue relationships are real. The same person calling the exhibitor services desk at Central Bank Center has been in the building enough times to know which approvals come back fast.

Across a multi-show calendar, the compounding effect of one team running show-site supervision is fewer surprises and shorter install times. The fourth show runs on memory; the first one runs on first-principles planning.

Show-Site Supervision planning questions

Do you work at Hyatt Regency Lexington?

Yes. the downtown Hyatt is part of the convention-hotel ring tied directly to the Central Bank Center campus. Projects at that building are planned against its specific floor rules.

How does labor work in Lexington?

It runs venue by venue. at the Campbell House Lexington, it offers more than 25,000 square feet of meeting and event space, which makes it a serious hospitality option for smaller conference and client-event programs. Other Lexington venues have their own published rules, and the plan matches the specific building.

How much lead time is ideal?

Custom builds run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rentals take 8 to 12 weeks. Work inside 4 weeks is possible but raises costs and narrows the options.

What information kicks off a project?

The show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and a target budget range. From those, an initial scope and a realistic cost come back within a few days.

Who runs the booth on-site?

One named show-site lead runs the install, the show hours, and the strike. That person carries the floor plan and the punch list from crates to doors and reports up once a day.

Can the booth travel to the next city on our calendar?

Yes — builds are designed for modular reuse unless single-use scenic is specifically required. Same structure, swappable graphics, standard crates. Lexington programs that roll to another city on the same calendar get handled end to end.

What happens if something breaks during show hours?

The on-site lead handles it. Minor repairs happen in place; anything larger gets sourced from the local shop or rerouted through the venue's service desk.
