Trade Show AV Production

Trade show AV production in Lexington covers the audio, video, lighting, and content path inside a booth or on a session stage, which means real cabling, real power loads, and content that has to play on cue.

AV scope ranges from a single monitor at an inline booth to a full LED wall, audio system, and speaker mic pack inside an island activation or general session at the convention center.

Power loads, cable paths, and rigging approvals come from the venue first. Central Bank Center publishes its rules and dimensions document; hotel ballrooms have their own house AV that may or may not align with what the program needs.

Content matters as much as hardware. Files have to be on the right format, the right resolution, and tested on the actual playback gear before doors open.

These pages cover what an AV production scope includes, what comes from house gear, and how the show-day operator handles live changes without breaking the run-of-show.

Trade Show AV Production priorities

  • Keep Trade Show AV Production tied to the actual show outcome, not a vague category label.
  • Treat the venue manual, freight timing, and access plan as the baseline for the build.
  • Align graphics, AV, and utility requests with the same calendar as the booth work.
  • Leave room for storage, reuse, and return freight so the next cycle starts cleanly.
  • Give the floor one clear lead so live decisions stay controlled during install and strike.

How Lexington floor operations work

Floor operations for trade show AV production follow the venue's posted rules. Central Bank Center publishes a current rules and dimensions document; downtown hotels distribute their own standards through the property events team.

Utility orders run through each venue's portal or order form, with discount cutoffs at 30 to 45 days before show open. Late orders cost more and may miss the install window.

Rigging approvals at Central Bank Center go through the venue's posted path; hotel ballrooms generally don't support rigging at the convention-center scale.

Labor calls coordinate with each venue's house team. Outside crews need EAC paperwork filed with the show GC or property before they can badge in.

What's included in the project scope

Scope on a trade show AV production engagement is downstream of the brief. The show, venue, audience, and date determine the line items.

AV scope covers monitors, LED, audio, lighting, content playback, on-site engineering, run-of-show operation, recording where requested, and a closeout that returns rented gear without backline charges.

Out of scope by default: show organizer fees, client travel and per diems, internal lead-capture and CRM tools, and vendor relationships the client manages directly.

On trade show AV production programs that repeat, asset management folds into the scope. Storage, refurb cycles, graphic rotation, and freight loop planning all join the engagement.

The biggest savings come from scope clarity at kickoff. Install-day surprises almost always trace back to scope gaps, not labor mistakes.

How a project runs

Phase one for trade show AV production is a real conversation about the show, the building, the booth, the calendar, and the budget shape.

After the brief, focus moves to the venue. Manual, dock, labor, rigging, utility orders all close out before drawings hit the shop.

With venue side closed, production turns on. The shop builds, the print runs, the AV gear pre-progs, the install plan drafts.

The install runs under one named lead. No rotating crew, no remote account manager: one person on the floor with full context.

Through show hours the lead stays. Anything that comes up at the booth (tech, sponsor, venue) handles without the client team leaving visitors.

Strike honors the venue's posted move-out, then a same-day debrief documents what held and what changes for the next show.

Pricing and scheduling

Pricing for trade show AV production scales with the venue's published rates, the booth's footprint, the freight pattern, and the labor calls. There's no single number that fits every program.

The variables that matter: structure (custom, modular, or rental), graphics, venue service fees, freight, supervision days, and any rigging or AV that pushes outside standard line items. Two trade show AV production programs with different footprints are different projects.

AV production runs against the show's run-of-show document, with content lockup typically 1 to 2 weeks before doors and gear pre-prog and load-in 1 to 3 days before show open.

What a successful project looks like

The shape of a good trade show AV production project: install on schedule, venue walk-through passes, show hours run without callouts, strike honors the posted window.

The numbers that matter on trade show AV production are leads, meetings, and pipeline. The booth and the install are inputs to those metrics.

Post-show, the floor data tells a better story than any pre-show model. The debrief documents what worked and what didn't.

Trade Show AV Production planning questions

Do you work at the Campbell House Lexington?

Yes. the Campbell House is a Lexington landmark just outside downtown with more than 25,000 square feet of meeting and event space. Projects at that building are planned against its specific floor rules.

How does labor work in Lexington?

It runs venue by venue. Central Bank Center: the dimensions and specification sheet makes the convention campus a real exhibit and conference environment with current floor and access considerations that should shape the brief early. Other Lexington venues have their own published rules, and the plan matches the specific building.

How much lead time is ideal?

Custom builds run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rentals take 8 to 12 weeks. Work inside 4 weeks is possible but raises costs and narrows the options.

What information kicks off a project?

The show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and a target budget range. From those, an initial scope and a realistic cost come back within a few days.

Who runs the booth on-site?

One named show-site lead runs the install, the show hours, and the strike. That person carries the floor plan and the punch list from crates to doors and reports up once a day.

Can the booth travel to the next city on our calendar?

Yes — builds are designed for modular reuse unless single-use scenic is specifically required. Same structure, swappable graphics, standard crates. Lexington programs that roll to another city on the same calendar get handled end to end.

What happens if something breaks during show hours?

The on-site lead handles it. Minor repairs happen in place; anything larger gets sourced from the local shop or rerouted through the venue's service desk.
