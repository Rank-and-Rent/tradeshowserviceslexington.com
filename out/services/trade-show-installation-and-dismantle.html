Trade Show Installation and Dismantle

Trade show installation and dismantle in Lexington is the on-floor labor work: uncrating, building, hanging graphics, integrating AV, and tearing the whole booth back down inside the venue's posted windows.

I&D runs against the venue's exhibitor manual and the show's published move-in and move-out schedule. Dock timing, crew calls, and rigging approvals all come from those documents.

At Central Bank Center the dock and rigging path is set by the venue's rules and dimensions document; hotel installs at Hyatt, Hilton, or Marriott City Center route through the in-house event team instead.

A clean install starts before the truck rolls: drawings with the crew, graphics labeled to position, hardware kits packed correctly, and a single named lead on the floor.

These pages cover the I&D scope, who does what during the install window, and how strike runs against the venue's posted dismantle hours.

Trade Show Installation and Dismantle priorities

  • Keep Trade Show Installation and Dismantle tied to the actual show outcome, not a vague category label.
  • Treat the venue manual, freight timing, and access plan as the baseline for the build.
  • Align graphics, AV, and utility requests with the same calendar as the booth work.
  • Leave room for storage, reuse, and return freight so the next cycle starts cleanly.
  • Give the floor one clear lead so live decisions stay controlled during install and strike.

Who handles what during the Lexington install window

Inside the install window for trade show installation and dismantle, the named on-site lead holds the drawing, the punch list, and the labor calls. That role doesn't rotate; it stays with one person from crate-open through final strike.

The venue's exhibitor services desk handles utility orders, rigging approvals, and the published service paperwork. The show GC (where there is one) handles drayage and the master schedule.

The labor crew runs against the venue's labor model. At Central Bank Center the crew works the building's published rules; at downtown hotels the crew works through the property's events team.

Communication runs on a daily report during install, real-time updates during show hours, and a written debrief delivered within a day of strike.

Scope on a trade show installation and dismantle project

An I&D scope for trade show installation and dismantle runs against the venue's exhibitor manual and the show's published move-in and move-out schedule.

The scope covers crate retrieval from drayage, uncrating, structural assembly, graphic application, AV and lighting installation, show-hours coverage, strike, re-crating, manifest handling, and outbound freight handoff.

One named on-site lead carries the install. The same person holds the drawing, the punch list, and the labor calls from crate-open through final strike.

Drayage and material handling are billed by the venue's GC. The I&D crew works the booth itself, not the freight movement on or off the dock.

I&D windows in Lexington match the show's published schedule, usually a 2 to 4 day load-in for full-floor exhibits and 4 to 8 hours for in-line booths.

Trade Show Installation and Dismantle planning questions

Do you work at Hilton Lexington/Downtown?

Yes. Hilton Lexington/Downtown sits steps from Rupp Arena and Lexington Center in the downtown core. Projects at that building are planned against its specific floor rules.

How does labor work in Lexington?

It runs venue by venue. at Marriott Lexington Griffin Gate Golf Resort & Spa, itself as a meeting and event hotel on Newtown Pike, which makes it relevant for retreat, hospitality, and overflow planning. Other Lexington venues have their own published rules, and the plan matches the specific building.

How much lead time is ideal?

Custom builds run best with 16 to 20 weeks of lead time. Rentals take 8 to 12 weeks. Work inside 4 weeks is possible but raises costs and narrows the options.

What information kicks off a project?

The show, the venue, the booth number, the dates, and a target budget range. From those, an initial scope and a realistic cost come back within a few days.

Who runs the booth on-site?

One named show-site lead runs the install, the show hours, and the strike. That person carries the floor plan and the punch list from crates to doors and reports up once a day.

Can the booth travel to the next city on our calendar?

Yes — builds are designed for modular reuse unless single-use scenic is specifically required. Same structure, swappable graphics, standard crates. Lexington programs that roll to another city on the same calendar get handled end to end.

What happens if something breaks during show hours?

The on-site lead handles it. Minor repairs happen in place; anything larger gets sourced from the local shop or rerouted through the venue's service desk.
